A day in the life of a stagehand
at Show Masters.

Published May 23, 2026 8 min read Show Masters Editorial

If you're thinking about applying to crew with us — or you've never run an arena load-in and want to know what you'd actually be doing — here it is. One representative show day, hour by hour, on a typical Orlando arena call. The pay, the hours, the Crew Lead structure, and what we ask of the people on the bench.

4:30am — call sheet pings

Your phone buzzes the night before with a Crew Lead's text and a PDF call sheet. Venue, dock entry, parking, gate code, show name, your role, your shift, your reporting time, your Crew Lead's name and cell. The call sheet is the call sheet — no surprises in the morning.

5:00am — coffee at the dock

You park, badge in at the venue's loading-dock gate, and find your Crew Lead under the work-lights. There's a coffee thermos on a road case and a hard-hat with your shift number on the table. The Crew Lead does a fast headcount, hands you your shift card, walks the crew through the call sheet, names the trim heights, calls out the day's hazards. Total: ten minutes. Then the call starts.

5:30am to 9:00am — load-in

The trucks back to the dock and we unload. As a stagehand, you'll be on the dock pulling road cases off the lift-gates, or on the deck staging them in the right zones, or running cable feeder to FOH. The Crew Lead is on radio coordinating between the four work zones — you'll hear them on your earpiece if you've been issued comms, otherwise they'll find you on foot.

The first three hours set the pace for the whole day. If we're sharp here, we finish early. If we're sloppy here, we miss meal break.

9:00am to noon — build

Truss goes up. Lighting hangs. Audio flies. Video comes out of cases. As a stagehand, you'll be wherever the Crew Lead routes you — ground-support for the riggers, cable runner for audio, gear-cage manager for video, or deck-build crew. Senior stagehands rotate into specialty work as they earn it. The Crew Lead watches and notes who picks up what.

Noon — meal break

30-minute lunch, on the clock. Catered when the venue has it, off-site when they don't (per diem covers it). You sit. You eat. You don't answer your radio for thirty minutes. The Crew Lead does — that's the job.

12:30pm to 5:00pm — focus and finish

Lighting on focus calls. Audio ringing out the room. Video patching and switcher tests. Your job during this stretch is cable management, deck dressing, hanging signage, dressing the FOH desk, prepping backstage areas. The Crew Lead does the hour-10 walk-through with you and the venue rep, naming what's still open. Anything you can fix in three minutes, you fix.

5:00pm — handoff to show crew

Production Manager arrives. The Crew Lead delivers the one-page handoff sheet: room status, open items, owner per item. You're on the call sheet for the show shift too — or you're off the clock for a few hours. Either way, the load-in is done.

9:00pm to midnight — show

If you're on show shift, you're holding spots, swapping deck setups between sets, standing by for scene changes, or running offstage with road cases for storage. If you're off-shift, you're at home with a hot meal. The Crew Lead is at the venue for the duration.

Midnight to 4:00am — load-out

Show ends. Strike begins immediately. This is the densest, fastest part of the day. Crew count is roughly 1.4x the load-in count because the venue's dock-access window is tight. As a stagehand, you'll be pulling cable, breaking down deck, road-casing gear, rolling cases out the dock, helping line up trucks. Crew Lead is on the radio coordinating five things at once. We finish on time. We always finish on time.

4:30am — sign out

You sign your shift card with your actual hours, hand it to the Crew Lead, get a fist-bump, and go home. Payday is Friday. The Crew Lead's sign-off goes to the office. Your hours are correct on the invoice because nobody fudges them — that's the whole point of the Crew Lead model.

What you actually make

A stagehand at Show Masters on a 2026 Orlando call bills out at $42–$58 per hour, and we pay the crew end of that range. Your effective W-2 wage for a 14-hour load-in/show day after taxes and benefits is roughly $310–$420, depending on shift premium, OT, and specialty premium. Lead Riggers and senior technicians make significantly more. Crew Leads make significantly more still.

What you also get: workers' comp on every shift, $2M GL on every shift, ACA-compliant health coverage offered if you're full-time, paid Crew Lead briefings, training paid for relevant certs (ETCP, SPRAT, OSHA-30, lift cert), and a Crew Lead who has your back when something goes wrong. You don't make any of that on a 1099 call, which is why we don't run 1099.

How to get on the bench

Apply through the careers page. If you have live-event experience, you go on the active bench right away. If you don't but you've worked an adjacent trade — electrician, construction carpenter, broadcast tech, home-theater installer — you go on the rookie bench and shadow a Crew Lead for your first two or three calls before getting your own assignments. We pay you for those shadow shifts. We get a lot of good crew that way.

The crew on the senior bench have been here for fifteen, twenty years. They make a living. Their kids went through school on it. That's the trade-off you sign up for: real wages, real benefits, a real career path. In exchange you show up on time, in dark clothes, with your own gloves, and you don't make the Crew Lead repeat themselves.
