Festival labor in Texas.
Austin vs. Houston.

Published Sep 1, 2026 10 min read Show Masters Editorial

Texas runs two distinct festival markets that producers often treat as one and then run into trouble when they don't. Austin is union-influenced, IATSE-adjacent, permit-tight, and operates on the calendar of a city that booked itself solid in 2010 and hasn't loosened up since. Houston is bigger, hotter, more permissive on the operations side, but has its own freight and labor patterns. Here is the operational primer.

The Austin market

Austin's festival scene is dominated by Austin City Limits Music Festival, South by Southwest, Austin Psych Fest, Levitation, and a long tail of mid-size outdoor productions at Zilker Park, the Long Center grounds, and the various ranch venues outside the city. The labor floor in Austin is significantly shaped by IATSE Local 205, which has long-standing jurisdictional relationships with most of the major venues and many of the festival production companies. That doesn't mean every job is union — it means you need to know which producers run union calls and which run non-union before you book.

Our take: roughly 60% of the high-profile Austin festival work is union-floor, 40% is non-union or mixed. When we crew Austin we usually fly in a small core from Dallas and supplement with non-union local hands. Where the show is union, we coordinate directly with Local 205's referral system rather than competing for the same bench.

The Houston market

Houston's festival market is mostly non-union and runs at a much larger physical scale. Free Press Summer Fest (paused recently), Day for Night, In Bloom, the rodeo concert series at NRG Stadium, the various corporate-private outdoor festivals at Discovery Green and Sam Houston Park — Houston runs a steady book of outdoor and stadium-adjacent festival work. The labor pool is larger than Austin's, the venues have wider freight access, and the operational tempo is closer to what we run in Dallas.

Our take: roughly 90% of the Houston festival work is non-union. We crew it from our Dallas hub plus a local Houston bench. The bench depth in Houston means we can run a 60-crew festival call without flying anyone in; in Austin the same call would need 15–25 traveling hands to round out the team.

Heat protocols — the thing nobody from out of state plans for

September in Austin is still summer. Heat index regularly exceeds 105°F during day-stage operation. Our heat protocols on Texas festival calls are not optional — we build them into the crew sheet from the brief stage:

  • Crew water: Two refillable water-station coolers per zone, restocked hourly during day shifts. We carry our own coolers because venue infrastructure is unreliable.
  • Mandatory shade breaks: 15 minutes every 90 minutes during 11am–5pm shifts. On the clock. Built into the budget.
  • Modified shift length: Day-stage shifts capped at 10 hours during high-heat days, vs. 12 hours in winter. Crew rotation is wider on hot days.
  • Heat-illness protocol: Crew Lead carries an electrolyte kit and a venue medic phone tree. We've called paramedics twice in the last five festival seasons; both crew recovered fully.
The single most common mistake out-of-state producers make on a Texas summer festival is under-crewing the load-in because they're used to cool-weather rates. We bid Texas summer at +20% crew count for the same physical scope.

Permit windows

Austin operates on a noise ordinance that limits amplified sound after 10pm on weeknights and 11pm on weekends in most park venues. That sounds like a small thing until you realize it means your load-out cannot include amplified system tests after curfew. Houston is more flexible — Discovery Green and Sam Houston Park both have late-night permit options that let load-out extend to 2am.

Practical implication: Austin festivals load out faster than they load in (no system tests on the way out), which compresses crew on strike. Houston festivals can stretch strike across a full overnight, which keeps the crew count steadier but extends the per-show payroll.

Freight access

Zilker Park is a logistical bottleneck. Trucks queue at Barton Springs Road, the venue access road is shared with public use, and freight elevators don't exist because most stages are ground-level builds. We've spent more time waiting on trucks at Zilker than at almost any other venue in our book.

NRG Park (the Houston equivalent for large festivals) has wider freight roads, dedicated production-load-in access, and night-shift freight windows. Operational pace at NRG Park is roughly 1.4x faster than Zilker for the same physical load-in volume.

Rigging differences

Austin's larger festival stages typically use proprietary stage decks from regional providers like Roc-N-Soc or Stageline. Those decks come with their own ground-rigging crews and have their own venue-specific quirks. We coordinate with the deck vendor's rigging team rather than running our own ground rig in most cases.

Houston festivals more often use independent rigging crews on standard truss + chain-motor configurations. Our ETCP-Rigger bench in Dallas is well-versed in these systems and we run our own rig on most Houston outdoor work.

The four things to ask before you book a Texas festival

  • Is the venue/show union-floor, non-union, or mixed jurisdiction?
  • What's the load-out curfew, and does it allow system tests during strike?
  • What's the heat-index projection for the load-in week, and what's the crew water/shade protocol budget?
  • Who's providing the stage decks, and is the ground-rigging crew separate from the AV crew?

If your producer hasn't asked these questions before sending you a budget, push back. Texas festival labor is more nuanced than "crew an outdoor show" suggests.
