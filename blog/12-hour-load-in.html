The 12-hour load-in.
What good crew leadership looks like.

Published May 5, 2026 8 min read Show Masters Editorial

A show stands or falls in the first three hours. By hour four, every problem you didn't catch in hour one has compounded into the kind of phone call producers remember six months later, when they're picking next year's vendor. The Crew Lead who knows what's coming — and who's on the floor when it does — is the difference between a clean call and a $40,000 over.

We have run 12-hour load-ins for 24 years. Most of them feel quiet. Some of them turn into a story. Either way, the difference is rarely the gear or the venue. It's the person holding the radio.

What a 12-hour load-in actually is

A "12-hour load-in" is the window from the moment the first truck backs to the loading dock to the moment the Production Manager calls the room ready for rehearsal. It's the bracket that includes truck unload, road-case stage, rigging up, deck build, audio fly, lighting hang and focus, video flat-pack, FOH build, cable runs, comms check, and the inevitable mid-call change order that comes in around hour seven.

On paper it's a sequence. In practice it's a negotiation between physical reality, the show's wish list, and the union or labor floor the venue enforces. The Crew Lead is the only person on the call who can see all three at once.

The first hour: setting the pace nobody talks about

Bad load-ins almost always start the same way. Trucks roll in late, the crew lingers near the dock waiting for direction, the first road case lands in the wrong spot, and by the time someone notices that fork two is feeding upstage instead of stage-left, you've burned twenty minutes you'll never recover.

A good Crew Lead starts the call before any case touches the deck. The walk-through with the venue rep happens in the parking lot, with the truck order sheet on a clipboard. The fork operators get the staging map before the first pallet drops. The crew gets paired off — two on each fork, two on the dock door, four on the deck — before they're standing around looking for an assignment.

The pace you set in the first hour is the pace you keep all day. If the call starts ambling, no amount of pushing at hour six will recover it.

The other thing that happens in hour one is the first round of small problems. A lift battery is dead. The freight elevator is locked. A trim height is wrong on the rigging plot. None of these are emergencies on their own. All of them become emergencies if they aren't named and assigned inside the first ninety minutes. A Crew Lead who walks the dock with a notepad and a head count solves three problems before the rest of the crew ever knows there were problems.

Hours two through four: the build

This is the part of the day producers picture when they think about "load-in" — the deck going up, the truss climbing, the lighting hanging. It looks busy on the timelapse. From the Crew Lead's chair, it's actually the calmest part of the day, because everything that's happening was planned. The crew knows their roles. The riggers are up. Audio is flying. Lighting is on focus calls. The Crew Lead's job during the build is mostly to watch for the things nobody planned for.

What we watch for, in roughly the order it usually breaks:

  • Rigging slippage. A trim that drifts two feet because the venue's measurement was off. Catch it before it forces a re-rig.
  • Deck weight. A staging plan that ignores point loads on a temporary deck. The deck holds. The platform underneath cracks.
  • Power. One circuit doing the job of three because the venue's panel labeling was wrong from a prior load-in.
  • Comms. A backstage runner with a dead radio. Twenty minutes of confusion before anyone notices nobody can hear them.
  • Time-of-day creep. Catering shows up late. A crew break stretches forty minutes. A union meal break starts before half the crew is ready to take it.

Each of these is a five-minute fix if you catch it as it's happening. Each becomes an hour of recovery if you don't catch it until it's a downstream problem.

The mid-call change order

Every show has one. Sometime between hour five and hour seven, somebody — the producer, the talent's manager, the venue, the agency — lands a new requirement. A second camera position. A different IEM mix. Truss that has to come down four feet because the talent wants to see the back of the room. A walk-on entrance the show designer added on the flight in.

The Crew Lead's job here is to do three things in this exact order. First, hear the request without flinching. Second, assess what it costs — in hours, in crew, in money, in the rest of the schedule. Third, name the trade-off out loud so the person asking can decide.

"Yes, we can take the truss down four feet. It's a re-rig — about ninety minutes with three riggers, which puts us into rehearsal a full hour late, and that probably moves dinner. Or we can leave the truss and reposition the seating — that's twenty minutes and we keep the schedule. Which way do you want to go?"

That answer makes the producer's job easier, not harder. The wrong answer — "we'll figure it out" — is the most expensive sentence anyone says on a load-in. It eats budget for the rest of the day and the Crew Lead loses the room.

Hours eight through eleven: the focus and the finish

By hour eight the build is mostly done and the show has moved from physical labor to detail work. Lighting is on focus and cue notes. Audio is gain-staging and ringing out the room. Video is patching, switching tests, IMAG calibration. The Crew Lead's job during this stretch is to keep the crew sharp without burning them out, because the last hour of any load-in is where the small things get missed.

Specifically: cable management gets sloppy when the crew is tired. Cases stop getting closed all the way. Toolboxes drift to the wrong cart. A spotlight position is left an inch off because somebody decided it was close enough. These are the things the producer notices at the run-of-show walk-through. They are also the things that cost nothing to do right the first time.

A good Crew Lead walks the room around hour ten. Quietly. With a Crew Lead from the venue if there is one. The walk catches what was missed, names it without drama, and gets it fixed before the production manager arrives.

Hour twelve: the handoff

The last hour is not really a load-in hour. It's a handoff. The Production Manager wants to know three things: is the room ready for rehearsal, what is still open, and who owns each open item. A Crew Lead who can answer those three questions cleanly in ninety seconds has just made the producer's day. A Crew Lead who has to flip through a notebook and check with three crew members has just told the producer something about how the rest of the show is going to go.

We train for the handoff explicitly. Every Crew Lead leaves load-in with a one-page list: room status, open items, owner per item, ETA per item, and a single sentence on each item the Production Manager would want to know but didn't ask about. The list goes to the PM, the venue rep, and the operations desk back at the office. It is the most important deliverable of the day.

The Crew Lead premium

We bill a Crew Lead at a higher rate than a stagehand. Producers occasionally ask why. The honest answer is that the Crew Lead is the difference between a $40,000 over and a clean invoice, and the math is so lopsided in favor of having one that it almost feels embarrassing to defend the line item.

A typical 30-crew load-in burns roughly $1,200 in Crew Lead hours over the 12-hour window. If the Crew Lead catches one rigging issue, one cable run, and one change-order assessment, the day pays for itself five times over before the rehearsal call. If the Crew Lead is also the person who keeps the same room booked with us the next time the producer is in market, the line item compounds for years.

This is the part of the job that doesn't show up in a marketing one-pager. It also doesn't show up on the dock cam. You only see it on the invoice at the end of the week, which arrives with no surprises — and on the calendar three months later, when the same producer calls back.

What to ask before you book your next 12-hour

When you call us — or anyone — for a load-in, the questions worth asking aren't about hourly rate or crew count. They're about how the Crew Lead is going to run the day. Ask:

  • Who is the Crew Lead and how long have they been with the company?
  • Will they walk the venue before the truck arrives?
  • What does the handoff at hour twelve look like?
  • What's the protocol when a mid-call change order lands?
  • Who carries the notebook at hour ten?

If the answers come back vague, that's the signal. If they come back specific, you're probably going to have a quiet day. A quiet day, in this business, is the highest compliment.
