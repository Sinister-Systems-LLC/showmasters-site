How much does stagehand labor
cost in 2026?

Published Jan 6, 2026 9 min read Show Masters Editorial

"What does a stagehand cost?" is the first question every producer asks. The honest answer is that it depends on four things, and once you know what they are, the spread between any two quotes you get this year stops being a mystery.

This piece walks through the math the same way we explain it on a discovery call. By the end you will know what to expect on an estimate, what the line items mean, and which questions to ask a vendor whose number looks too good to be true.

The headline number

In 2026, an experienced stagehand on a non-union live-event call in Florida or Texas bills between $42 and $58 per hour, all-in. A Lead Rigger or Crew Lead bills between $72 and $95. Specialist technicians — audio engineers, lighting programmers, video TDs — bill between $85 and $145 depending on the show and the specialty.

Those ranges are wide because they bake in the variables. A two-hand corporate breakout for a returning client in Orlando costs us less to staff than a 38-crew arena keynote in Austin during festival week. The same person bills differently in those two contexts. The good news is that once you understand the four levers underneath the rate, you can predict where in the range your show will land before you ever request a quote.

What's in the hourly rate (and what isn't)

This is where most pricing confusion lives. A "stagehand rate" from one vendor and a "stagehand rate" from another can look like the same number on a one-pager and produce wildly different invoices, because the two vendors include different things in the rate.

What we include in ours, every line, every shift:

  • Workers' compensation insurance. Required in all 50 states. Roughly $4 to $7 per hour per crew member, more in high-risk roles like rigging.
  • General liability insurance. A $2M aggregate policy on every show, additional insured certificates on request, no upcharge.
  • Payroll taxes. FICA, FUTA, SUTA, plus state-specific add-ons. Roughly 8 to 12 percent of gross wages, depending on state.
  • ACA-compliant benefits. Health, dental, basic vision for full-time crew. Required for employers above 50 FTE and we elect into it below that line because it's why the senior crew stays.
  • Crew Lead time. Pre-call planning, venue walk-through, post-load handoff. Roughly 1.5 to 2 hours per show that you would otherwise be charged hourly.
  • Travel and per diem on relevant calls. Truck driving time when crew is moving between venues, lodging when we're traveling crew to your event.

What is not in our hourly rate (and shouldn't be in anyone's):

  • Equipment and consumables (gaff tape, cable, batteries).
  • Per diem on multi-day road shows when you're providing meals.
  • Production gear (lighting fixtures, audio systems, video walls).
  • Hotel and air for non-local crew.
  • Permit fees and venue-required fees.

If a vendor quotes you a rate that seems 25 percent under market, the question to ask is which of the included items above are actually excluded. Most often the answer is workers' comp (offloaded to you), payroll taxes (because they're paying 1099, which is a separate problem we wrote about in this piece), or insurance limits below what your venue requires.

The four levers that move the rate

Within the $42 to $58 range for a stagehand, where you land depends on four things. We rank them by impact.

1. Role and certification

A stagehand without specialty certifications bills at the low end of the range. A rigger with ETCP certification bills at the high end of the rigger range — not because we mark them up, but because they cost more to retain. The same person can carry a forklift certification, a SPRAT rope-access cert, an OSHA-30, or a state-specific lighting-electrician license, and each one adds a few dollars to their effective hourly because we pay them more to hold the cert.

2. Geography

Orlando and Dallas, where we have offices, are the cheapest places to crew with us. Other markets where we have hub-level relationships — Tampa, Houston, Atlanta, Nashville, Denver, Las Vegas, Los Angeles — are roughly 5 to 12 percent higher because crew travel adds to the math. Markets outside that footprint require either flying core crew with you (which we do regularly) or sourcing through a vetted local partner, which adds a small coordination fee but keeps the rate competitive.

3. Show timing

A weekday daytime call in our home market is the base rate. Nights, weekends, holidays, and overnight calls add 10 to 50 percent depending on local labor law. A call that pushes into overtime — over 40 hours in the week, or over 8 hours per day in daily-OT states like California — bills the OT hours at 1.5x. Holiday OT bills at 2.0x. We break OT projections out on every estimate, so the invoice never surprises you.

4. Show complexity

A keynote in a ballroom with a single screen and a podium is simple. A festival with three stages, a follow-spot tower, and a dynamic rigging plot is not. Complex shows need more senior crew, more Crew Lead time, more pre-call planning. The hourly rate per role doesn't change — but the mix does, with more Lead Riggers and Specialist Technicians and fewer entry-level hands. The blended rate goes up.

The single biggest predictor of total labor cost is not the hourly rate. It's the crew sheet — how many people, in which roles, for how many hours.

This is why two vendors can quote the same hourly and produce 30 percent different totals. Ask any vendor to walk you through the crew sheet line by line. The vendor who can do it without checking a notebook is the one who has actually thought about your show.

A worked example

Let's price a representative two-day corporate plenary at a downtown Orlando hotel ballroom. 800 attendees, single general session, four breakouts. Here is the crew sheet we would build:

  • 1 Crew Lead — 12 hr/day × 2 days at $82 = $1,968
  • 2 Lead Riggers — 6 hr load-in only at $88 = $1,056
  • 2 Lighting Technicians — 12 hr/day × 2 at $95 = $4,560
  • 2 Audio Technicians — 12 hr/day × 2 at $95 = $4,560
  • 1 Video TD — 12 hr/day × 2 at $115 = $2,760
  • 6 Stagehands — 12 hr/day × 2 at $48 = $6,912
  • 1 Forklift Operator — 6 hr load-in + 4 hr load-out at $58 = $580

Subtotal: $22,396. OT projection for the load-in/load-out days: roughly $1,800. Estimated total: $24,196. That's all labor, with insurance, taxes, benefits, and Crew Lead time baked in. No admin percentage. No surprises on the invoice.

For comparison, a vendor running 1099 contractors at a "cheaper" rate might quote $19,500 for the same show. They are not actually cheaper. They are offloading workers' comp, payroll taxes, and benefits onto you (or onto the crew, who then leave the industry after their first injury). The total cost to the producer, after a misclassification audit or a venue refusing the COI, is dramatically higher. Ask any production manager who has been through that.

What to ask before you sign

Five questions that separate honest pricing from the other kind:

  • Are your crew W-4 employees or 1099 contractors? (W-4 is the only correct answer.)
  • Is workers' comp and GL included in the hourly rate? (Yes is the only correct answer.)
  • What are your aggregate insurance limits? ($2M aggregate / $1M per occurrence is industry standard.)
  • How do you handle overtime — weekly, daily, or both? (Per state law is the only correct answer.)
  • What does the estimate-to-invoice variance look like on your last 20 jobs? (Under 5 percent is the answer that matters.)

If a vendor can answer those five clearly and specifically, you are talking to a real operator. If the answers are vague or evasive, you are about to find out something on the invoice that the estimate didn't tell you.
