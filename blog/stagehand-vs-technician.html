Stagehand vs. technician.
What's the difference?

Published Feb 3, 2026 7 min read Show Masters Editorial

The two words get used interchangeably on a lot of estimates. They shouldn't be. A stagehand and a technician do different work, cost different money, and need different training. If your invoice lumps them together, you are almost certainly being charged for one role at the rate of the other — and it usually isn't in your favor.

Here is the line we draw, why we draw it, and how to read it on a quote.

The one-sentence version

Stagehands move it, build it, and tear it down. Technicians own the systems that run during the show. Both are on payroll. Both are W-4 employees. Both are insured. But one is paid for muscle and crew-skill, and the other is paid for specialized knowledge of audio, lighting, video, or stage automation under live conditions.

What a stagehand actually does

A stagehand is the crew member who turns an empty room into a show. The job covers everything physical: truck unload, road-case staging, deck build, truss assembly on the ground, cable runs, set pieces, props placement, drape hang, signage placement. During the show, stagehands hold spots, handle scene-change moves, swap deck setups between sessions, and stand by for anything that requires hands.

It is skilled labor. A senior stagehand reads load-in plots, understands rigging vocabulary, knows the difference between a quad box and a multi, and can build a backline in twenty minutes flat. But the work is fundamentally physical and is paid as such. A stagehand on a 2026 call in our market bills $42 to $58 per hour, all-in.

What a technician actually does

A technician owns one of the show's systems and is responsible for it from load-in to load-out. The four common technician disciplines:

  • Audio. An audio engineer mixes the show or runs monitors. They tune the system to the room, ring out feedback, manage wireless coordination, set up shotgun and lavalier mics for keynote talent, and run the desk during the call.
  • Lighting. A lighting programmer (sometimes called an LD or "the designer") builds cue stacks, programs moving lights, focuses fixtures, and runs the console during the show. A separate "follow-spot operator" sits at a spot and operates it — that role can be a tech or a senior stagehand depending on complexity.
  • Video. A video TD (technical director) runs the switcher, manages camera operators, programs graphics workflow, and coordinates with the producer in headset during the show. Adjacent roles: playback operator, IMAG camera operator, projectionist.
  • Stage automation. On larger shows, an automation tech runs the motorized scenery — tracking turntables, lifts, deck wagons, fly automation cues. Specialty role; rare below arena scale.

Technicians bill $85 to $145 per hour depending on discipline, experience, and gear they bring with them. Lighting programmers at the high end carry their own consoles. Audio engineers at the high end carry their own RF coordination tools. The rate reflects the equipment as much as the labor.

Where the confusion comes from

Several reasons the two roles get bundled in casual conversation. The two big ones:

  • Many technicians started as stagehands. The career path is well-worn, and an experienced lighting tech can still run cable like a stagehand — they just don't bill stagehand rate for it.
  • Smaller vendors quote a single blended rate to make the estimate look simple. That rate is usually the technician rate, applied to the stagehands. Or worse, it's the stagehand rate, applied to the technicians (which is how you end up with an audio engineer who has never tuned a system running your show).

Either way, the producer pays a mix-rate, doesn't see the breakdown, and discovers on day one that the "lighting tech" the vendor sent is actually a stagehand who has been pushed into a chair he's never sat in.

The biggest tell is the line item. A real production logistics vendor breaks out stagehand hours, technician hours, and Crew Lead hours separately on the estimate. Vendors who hide the mix are usually hiding something.

How it should appear on your invoice

Every estimate we send has three blocks. Stagehand hours by shift, technician hours by discipline, and Crew Lead hours. Each block has a count, an hourly rate, and a subtotal. Overtime projections are called out separately.

You should see something like this:

  • 4 Stagehands × 12 hr × $48 = $2,304
  • 2 Lighting Techs × 12 hr × $95 = $2,280
  • 1 Audio Engineer × 12 hr × $115 = $1,380
  • 1 Crew Lead × 14 hr × $82 = $1,148
  • OT projection: ~$420

That estimate tells you exactly what you're paying for. If the estimate you have in front of you doesn't, ask the vendor to send a version that does. The good ones will. The ones who can't are the ones you should worry about.

Why we classify and bill them separately

Two reasons. One: it's honest. Stagehand work and technician work are not the same product, and pretending otherwise misleads the producer. Two: it lets the producer scale the show without overpaying. On a small breakout where you don't need a programmer, you don't pay for one — you pay for a stagehand who can sub-rig the rep plot. On a complex keynote where you need a real lighting designer, you pay for one and don't get a stagehand who's hoping to figure it out.

Some vendors push back on this and quote a single rate because it's easier to defend and easier to mark up. We don't, because most of our work is repeat business, and producers who get a transparent estimate the first time book again.

One last note on overlap

There is one zone where stagehand and technician work blur cleanly, and it's worth naming. On a 12-hour load-in, half the day is physical (load-in, truss build, cable, focus) and half is technical (programming, tuning, rehearsing). Many of our senior crew can move between both, and they often do. We bill the technical hours at technical rate and the physical hours at stagehand rate — from the same person, in the same day — because that's what actually happened. It looks weird on the invoice the first time you see it. It's also accurate, which is the whole point.
