Ten questions to ask
before you book a crewing company.

Published Nov 3, 2026 9 min read Show Masters Editorial

A smart Production Manager spends fifteen minutes on a discovery call before signing anything with a new crewing partner. These are the ten questions to open with — and the answers that should make you walk away. We get asked these ourselves; we wrote down what good answers sound like, and what bad answers tell you about the vendor.

1. Are your crew W-4 employees or 1099 contractors?

Good answer: "Every staffer is a W-4 employee on our payroll. We don't run 1099 contractors. Workers' comp, GL, and payroll taxes are baked into the hourly."

Walk-away answer: "Most are 1099. We can run a mix if you need it." That is the answer of a company you don't want on your call sheet. 1099 misclassification on a live-event crew is the kind of compliance gap that lands on the producer's invoice once the auditor shows up. See why classification matters.

2. Can you send a sample COI before we sign?

Good answer: "Yes, today. $2M aggregate general liability, statutory workers' comp, auto, and umbrella, with your company and the venue named as additional insured. Waiver of subrogation included." Sample COI arrives in your inbox within the hour.

Walk-away answer: "We can get you something." Vague COI talk is almost always a vendor whose policy has gaps they don't want to commit to in writing.

3. Who is the Crew Lead on this show, and how long have they been with you?

Good answer: A specific name, tenure, and the venues they've worked at. "Mike — eight years on our bench. He's run OCCC plenaries roughly twenty times. I'll connect you on email so you can ask him anything."

Walk-away answer: "We'll assign one closer to the show." Crew Lead continuity is the single biggest variable in whether a show runs smoothly. A vendor that doesn't have a named Crew Lead at booking is a vendor that hasn't built a stable bench.

4. What's your response time on a new estimate?

Good answer: "Same business day on weekdays. Phone or email, your choice. After-hours calls for active loads go to our orders desk."

Walk-away answer: "We aim for 48 hours." A 48-hour estimate response on a same-week show means the vendor is either understaffed on the sales side or doesn't have systems to respond quickly. Both are predictors of how they'll handle a real production crisis.

5. What does the estimate-to-invoice variance look like on your last 20 jobs?

Good answer: A real number, in the 3–8% range for change-orders, with examples. "Average variance is 4.6% on our last 20 jobs. The biggest single overage was a 14% on a show where the producer added a second IMAG zone day-of. We can talk through how we communicate change-orders during the show."

Walk-away answer: "We hit our estimates." A vendor that can't quote a real variance number doesn't actually track this. They're going to surprise you on the invoice.

6. How do you handle overtime?

Good answer: "Per state law. Florida and Texas: weekly OT at 1.5x over 40 hours. California-style markets: daily OT at 1.5x over 8 hours. We project OT on every estimate so there are no surprises."

Walk-away answer: "OT bills separately." Means it's not in the estimate. Means the invoice will be 10–25% higher than the quote.

7. What are your rigging certifications?

Good answer: "Lead Riggers and up-riggers carry ETCP-Rigger. Some carry SPRAT for rope-access work. Ground riggers are OSHA Fall-Protection trained. Every rig is signed off by a Lead Rigger before show-call."

Walk-away answer: "Our riggers are experienced." Experience isn't certification. ETCP and SPRAT exist because experience alone doesn't catch every failure mode. A vendor without certified riggers is also a vendor whose insurance may exclude rigging work at height. Verify both.

8. Will I work with the same Crew Lead next time I book?

Good answer: "Yes — we track preferred Crew Leads on every account and route your next booking to the same person by default. Most of our repeat clients book the same Crew Lead for years."

Walk-away answer: "We'll do our best." The Crew Lead bench is either stable or it isn't. A vendor that can't commit to continuity is a vendor whose senior crew has high turnover, which is a predictor of show-day quality variability.

9. How fast do you send the invoice, and how detailed is it?

Good answer: "Within 72 hours of load-out. Line-itemed by role, count, hours, and rate. OT broken out separately. Net-30 standard, custom terms by request."

Walk-away answer: "Within two weeks." Two weeks of invoice lag is a vendor's bookkeeping system telling you it isn't reconciled. Often a sign that the vendor's pricing was approximate, not contractual, and they're back-solving the labor numbers.

10. What's your cancellation policy?

Good answer: "Free cancellation up to 14 days before load-in. Inside 14 days, 25% of the labor estimate. Inside 72 hours, full labor charge — the crew turned down other work."

Walk-away answer: "We don't really have one." Means there's no contractual structure. Means a difficult cancellation conversation will get expensive, fast.

None of these questions are gotchas. They're standard. A reputable crewing partner will answer all ten clearly and specifically in fifteen minutes. If a vendor stumbles on three or more, you have your answer.

The bonus question nobody asks

"Can I talk to a past client?" The honest answer is: yes, here are three references with their direct phone numbers, all in the last twelve months. If the vendor's reference list is older than two years or includes only first names, that tells you something about their book of business.
