From stagehand to Crew Lead.
The career path here.

Published May 23, 2026 9 min read Show Masters Editorial

Most live-event crew companies don't have a career ladder. You sign on as a stagehand, you stay a stagehand, you eventually take a different job. Show Masters does it differently. Here is the actual ladder — six rungs from "first call" to "running the company's biggest shows" — with the years, the pay, and what every rung actually requires.

Rung 1 · Apprentice / Rookie Stagehand

Year 0 to year 1. You're on the bench but you don't run point on anything yet. You shadow a senior stagehand on your first two or three calls (paid), then start getting your own assignments on the simpler shifts — convention move-in, hotel-ballroom load-in, smaller corporate plenaries.

Pay: $18–$22/hr starting. W-4, workers' comp, GL, ACA benefits available at full-time hours.

What you need: Show up on time. Read a call sheet. Lift 50 lbs. Take direction without making your Crew Lead repeat themselves. We hire rookies from related trades (electrician, construction carpenter, broadcast tech, home-theater installer) all the time.

Rung 2 · Stagehand

Year 1 to year 3 (typically). You know the venues we crew. You've worked all the major show-types — corporate, concert, festival, trade show, theater, worship. Crew Leads request you by name on their next call. You're starting to pick up specialty hours (cable runner for audio, ground rig for the rigging team, deck-build crew).

Pay: $24–$32/hr. OT 1.5x on weekly or daily-OT depending on state. Specialty premium $2–$4/hr where applicable.

What you need: Two to three years of clean call sheets. No-show rate under 2%. Positive feedback from two or more Crew Leads. Most stagehands stay at this rung for a stable, well-paid career; the rungs above are optional.

Rung 3 · Specialty Crew (Tech / Rigger / Lift)

Year 2+ (track-specific). You've picked a specialty and we have certified you on it. Audio engineer with a console preference. Lighting programmer comfortable on grandMA or HOG. Lead Rigger with ETCP/SPRAT. Lift operator with OSHA-30. Camera operator on broadcast multicam.

Pay: $42–$95/hr depending on specialty. Riggers and senior technicians are the high end. We pay for cert renewals on our budget; see why we pay riggers more.

What you need: The cert (or willingness to test for it). Show-time discipline. Specialty pre-call planning. Most crew at this rung also work some standard stagehand shifts on their non-specialty days — the schedule blends.

Rung 4 · Crew Lead

Year 7+ typically. The Crew Lead is the most important role in the company. One per shift, runs the floor, carries the radio, signs the shift cards, handles the mid-call change-order conversations. We promote almost every Crew Lead from inside the company because the role requires deep familiarity with our standards, our venues, and our producers.

Pay: $58–$88/hr. Pre-call walk-through hours paid. Often plus a small per-show retainer. Senior Crew Leads with stadium-scale credits regularly clear six figures annually as W-2 income.

What you need: Seven-plus years of clean shifts at the stagehand or specialty rung. Reads people and reads plots equally well. Calm under cue. Internal-promotion preferred — we promote from inside roughly 90% of the time. We also occasionally hire external Crew Leads from peer companies; the path there is faster but rarer.

The Crew Lead is the single biggest variable in whether a show goes smoothly or doesn't. We invest in this role accordingly.

Rung 5 · Stage Manager

Year 9+ typically. Specialist Crew Lead role for theater, concert tour, multi-day corporate plenary, or long-run residency. The Stage Manager handles cue-calling, run-of-show ownership, dressing-room coordination, talent management, and the production-meeting relationships. Some Crew Leads transition into Stage Manager full-time; others rotate between the two roles.

Pay: $68–$110/hr. Multi-day per diem on traveling residencies. Many shows have a Stage Manager booked for the duration of the run.

What you need: Cue-calling background or theater training. The temperament for high-touch shows. Some of our Stage Managers came up through the theater side directly; others crossed over from Crew Lead.

Rung 6 · Technical Director

Year 12+ typically. The senior-most field role. The TD owns the full technical scope of a show across departments — audio, lighting, video, rigging, automation — and often acts as the production-side technical lead the producer talks to. On our larger arena and stadium calls, the TD coordinates between our Crew Lead and the producer's PM. A small bench of TDs at the company.

Pay: $115–$165/hr. Frequently includes pre-production hours and post-show closeout hours billed at a stepped-down rate. Senior TDs work salaried equivalent at well into six figures.

What you need: Deep cross-department fluency. Twelve-plus years in production. Often a prior Stage Manager or senior Crew Lead role. We have promoted internally and hired in from peer companies in roughly equal measure at this rung.

Adjacent paths

Not everyone wants the floor-running track. Two adjacent paths run alongside:

  • Personnel Coordinator / Operations. Office-based. Schedule the bench, match crew to calls, run the inbox during show weeks. Salaried, promotion-track into Operations Director. Hired from the floor bench (rare) or directly (common).
  • Account Sales / Production Relationships. Office + travel. The producer-facing relationships. Salaried + commission on repeat-book retention. Almost always hired in from peer agencies or from senior-crew bench at companies where they've built producer relationships already.

What we ask of crew on the track

Three things, in order of importance:

  • Show up. No-show rates above 2% per quarter take you off the active bench. Repeated lateness same. Producers book the company because they trust the call sheet; we keep that trust by treating it as inviolable.
  • Don't make your Crew Lead repeat themselves. Listen the first time. Read the call sheet. Read the room. If you're not sure, ask once.
  • Don't lie on your shift card. Sign the actual hours you worked. We catch fudging and it ends the career here. We pay the actual hours, every time, without question, because we trust the card.

That's the deal. The rest — the cert support, the benefits, the promotion track, the Crew Lead bench backing you up — we provide.

How to start
