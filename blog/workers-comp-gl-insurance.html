Workers' comp and GL insurance.
What to expect from your crewing partner.

Published Mar 3, 2026 10 min read Show Masters Editorial

Producers rarely lose sleep over the insurance certificate until the day they need to look at one. By that day, it's too late. This piece walks through what a real Certificate of Insurance from a crewing partner should include, the policy limits that matter, and the three exclusions that quietly leave the producer holding the bag.

If you book live event labor, this is the chapter most worth reading carefully.

The two policies that matter

Two coverages do the heavy lifting on a live event call. Workers' compensation, which protects the crew if they get hurt on the job. General liability, which protects the producer and the venue if the crew damages property or injures a third party.

Every reputable crewing partner carries both. Every reputable crewing partner includes them in the hourly rate. Every reputable crewing partner provides the certificate at no charge, in the producer's name, with the venue named as additional insured on request. If any of those three things is not happening, you are not working with a reputable crewing partner.

Workers' compensation: what it does and why it matters to you

Workers' comp covers the crew. If a stagehand drops a road case on a foot, or a rigger takes a fall, the policy pays the medical bills and lost wages. The crew member cannot sue the employer. The employer cannot pass the cost to the producer. That's the whole point of the policy.

The reason it matters to the producer: in the absence of workers' comp coverage, an injured worker has nowhere to go except the deepest pockets they can reach. That's the producer, or the venue, or both. Even if you win the lawsuit, you've spent six months and a substantial legal budget defending yourself for an injury that should have been a routine claim against an existing policy.

Workers' comp is required in 49 of 50 states (Texas is the exception — opt-in for most employers, but every crewing partner worth talking to carries it anyway). Required minimum limits vary by state. Our policy carries statutory limits in every state we crew, with employer liability at $1M per occurrence.

General liability: what it does

GL covers third parties. If a crew member rolls a forklift through a $50,000 video wall, or a piece of truss falls and damages the venue's chandelier, or an attendee trips over a cable run and breaks an arm, GL pays the claim. It also covers products liability and personal injury — the categories that catch people off guard.

Standard industry limits are $1M per occurrence and $2M aggregate. Some venues require $3M or $5M aggregate, which is straightforward to bump for a specific event with an excess umbrella policy. Ask the vendor whether they carry an umbrella. The answer should be yes — we carry $5M excess on top of the $2M primary.

What a real COI looks like

The Certificate of Insurance (ACORD form 25) lists carrier names, policy numbers, effective dates, coverage types, and limits. A real COI from a crewing partner will include all of these:

  • Workers' compensation, statutory limits, employer liability $1M/$1M/$1M.
  • General liability, $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate, products-completed-ops aggregate $2M, personal and advertising injury $1M.
  • Automobile liability, $1M combined single limit (for crew transport, forklifts, lift equipment).
  • Umbrella / excess, $5M+.
  • Additional insured endorsement naming the producer and the venue.
  • Waiver of subrogation in favor of the additional insured.
  • Primary and non-contributory wording.

That last three lines — additional insured, waiver of subrogation, primary and non-contributory — are what move the risk from the producer to the crewing partner where it belongs. If a vendor sends a COI without those endorsements, the producer is still exposed even though the policies exist.

Ask for the endorsements, not just the COI. A clean COI with weak endorsements is a piece of paper that won't actually cover you when something goes wrong.

The three exclusions that quietly leave producers exposed

Most GL policies in the live-event sector carry standard ISO exclusions, plus a few that are common in the industry but worth knowing about. Three exclusions to ask about specifically:

1. Rigging / aerial work exclusions

Some carriers exclude work above a certain height, or work involving overhead rigging, unless the policy explicitly schedules it. If your show involves chain motors, truss flown overhead, or any work above 8 feet, ask the vendor to confirm that rigging is not excluded on their primary or umbrella. If it is, the producer absorbs the rigging liability.

2. Pyrotechnics, lasers, and special effects

Universally excluded from standard GL. If your show has SFX, you need a specialty rider or a separately insured SFX subcontractor. Our crewing partner role does not include SFX coverage — we coordinate with licensed SFX vendors who carry their own policies and provide their own COI.

3. Subcontracted labor

If a vendor subcontracts hands to your show through a third party, ask whether the GL policy extends to subcontractors. Many policies don't. If a subcontracted hand drops something on your show, the producer can end up in a chain of indemnification that takes a year to resolve. We W-4 every crew member on our payroll and don't subcontract labor on standard calls — the entire crew is covered under our primary.

The 1099 problem

The single most common insurance gap in the industry is the vendor who quotes "stagehand rates" but pays 1099 contractors. By IRS definition, a 1099 contractor is not an employee, and the vendor's workers' comp policy generally does not extend to non-employees. If the 1099 hand gets hurt, the policy doesn't cover them, the vendor isn't liable (because the contractor is "independent"), and the injured worker's only recourse is to sue the producer or the venue.

This is the most expensive insurance gap in the industry. It looks like a 15 percent discount on the estimate. It can land as a six-figure lawsuit on the producer twelve months later. We wrote about it specifically in this piece.

What to ask before you sign

Six questions, in this order:

  • Are crew W-4 employees on your payroll, or 1099 contractors?
  • What are your aggregate GL limits, and do you carry an umbrella?
  • Can I see a sample COI with the producer named as additional insured?
  • Does the policy include waiver of subrogation and primary & non-contributory wording?
  • Is rigging covered on your primary, or is there a height exclusion?
  • Have you had a claim in the last five years, and if so, how did it close out?

The last question is the most revealing. A vendor who has been in business for ten years and "has never had a claim" is either lying or has never run a show with real risk. The right answer is something like: "We've had three claims in five years. One forklift incident closed at $14k, one cable trip closed at $7k, one slip-and-fall closed at $22k. All within policy, no impact on the producer." That's an honest operator.
