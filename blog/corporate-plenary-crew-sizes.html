Corporate plenary crew sizes.
The math by attendee count.

Published Jul 7, 2026 8 min read Show Masters Editorial

Producers ask us "how many crew do I need" before they ask anything else. The honest answer depends on attendee count plus a few production multipliers. Here is the math we use when a producer drops a brief and asks for a same-day estimate.

The baseline framework

Corporate plenaries roughly cluster into five size tiers. Each tier has a baseline crew sheet that scales predictably, with multipliers for complexity. The five tiers:

  • Breakout-only: 50–200 attendees, hotel ballroom, single screen, podium mic.
  • Small plenary: 200–500 attendees, single GS room, IMAG, basic lighting.
  • Mid plenary: 500–1,500 attendees, GS + 4–6 breakouts, full LED lighting, multi-camera IMAG, streaming.
  • Large plenary: 1,500–3,000 attendees, GS + 6–10 breakouts, full production rig, multi-day, streaming + hybrid.
  • Stadium-scale: 3,000–5,000+ attendees, arena or convention center main hall, multi-screen video walls, full broadcast.

Tier 1 — Breakout-only (50–200 attendees)

Typical crew: 2–4 hands total.

  • 1 stagehand / general AV
  • 1 Crew Lead (doubles as A2 / runner)
  • 0–2 stagehands on load-in / strike only

Hours: 4-hour load-in, 4-hour show, 2-hour strike = ~10 crew-hours per crew member, 30–40 total crew-hours.

Typical labor cost: $1,800–$3,500 depending on market.

Tier 2 — Small plenary (200–500 attendees)

Typical crew: 6–10 hands across the day.

  • 4 stagehands
  • 1 audio tech
  • 1 lighting tech (often combined with audio at this scale)
  • 1 video tech (handles IMAG switcher)
  • 1 Crew Lead
  • 1 stage manager for run-of-show (if program is complex)

Hours: 8-hour load-in, 6-hour show, 4-hour strike = 18 hours per crew member, 110–180 total.

Typical labor cost: $6,500–$11,000.

Tier 3 — Mid plenary (500–1,500 attendees)

The single most common corporate event tier. The crew sheet starts to bifurcate between the GS team and the breakout team.

Typical crew: 18–28 hands.

  • 6–10 stagehands (split between GS and breakout build)
  • 2 Lead Riggers + 2 ground riggers (truss for lighting + IMAG)
  • 1–2 lift operators
  • 2 audio techs (A1 + A2)
  • 2 lighting techs (programmer + spot/follow)
  • 2 video techs (switcher + camera shader/playback)
  • 1 video TD or Director
  • 1 Stage Manager
  • 1–2 Crew Leads (one for GS, one floating)

Hours: 12–14 hour load-in day, 8–10 hour show day(s), 6-hour strike. Total: 350–600 crew-hours for a single-day show, 700–1,100 for a two-day show.

Typical labor cost: $22,000–$55,000 for a 1-2 day mid plenary.

Tier 4 — Large plenary (1,500–3,000 attendees)

At this scale, the GS room is its own production and breakouts become significant secondary productions. Multi-day is the norm, not the exception. Streaming + hybrid is universal.

Typical crew: 30–45 hands.

  • 12–15 stagehands across all rooms
  • 3–4 Lead Riggers + 3 up-riggers + 3 ground riggers
  • 2–3 lift operators
  • 3–4 audio techs (A1 GS, A1 breakouts, A2 GS, RF coordinator)
  • 3 lighting techs (programmer, FOH op, follow-spot)
  • 4–6 video techs (multi-cam shaders, multi-zone switchers, playback, IMAG director)
  • 1 video TD
  • 1–2 Stream/Hybrid techs (Zoom/Teams/proprietary platform ops)
  • 2 Stage Managers (GS + breakout)
  • 2–3 Crew Leads

Hours: 14-hour load-in day, two 10-hour show days, 6-hour strike. Total: 1,400–2,200 crew-hours over 4-day window.

Typical labor cost: $85,000–$160,000 for a 3-4 day large plenary.

Tier 5 — Stadium scale (3,000–5,000+ attendees)

At this scale you're moving into shared crewing territory with concert tour-stop production. Full broadcast capability, multi-cam director track, video walls measured in square footage rather than panel count.

Typical crew: 50–75+ hands.

  • 20–30 stagehands
  • 4–6 Lead Riggers (stadium + arena rigs)
  • 6–10 up-riggers and ground riggers
  • 4 lift operators
  • 6–8 audio techs (full broadcast audio chain)
  • 6–8 lighting techs
  • 10+ video techs (broadcast multi-cam, multi-screen ops, playback, graphics)
  • 1–2 Technical Directors
  • 2–3 Stage Managers
  • 3–4 Crew Leads

Hours: 14-hour load-in, 10-hour show day, 6-hour strike. 2,500–4,500+ crew-hours.

Typical labor cost: $180,000–$425,000+ for a single major-show event.

The four multipliers

The baseline above assumes a "standard" corporate plenary. Four common factors push crew counts and hours up from baseline:

1. Streaming / hybrid (+15–25%)

Adds a streaming director, encoder operator, and remote-audience producer. Often adds a second camera operator dedicated to broadcast framing. Material at Tier 3+.

2. Pre-program rehearsals (+20–30%)

If the show has a tech rehearsal day before the GS day, plan on running the full crew for that rehearsal day. The cheapest way to under-crew a show is to skip rehearsal and discover what's broken during the live program.

3. Talent management (+1–3 hands)

Wireless coordination for 5+ wireless mic packs needs a dedicated RF coordinator. Multi-VIP shows with green-room runners need 1–2 stagehands assigned specifically to talent.

4. Multi-room concurrency (+30–60%)

Six concurrent breakouts mean six rooms of crew running simultaneously. The cheapest way to under-crew a show is to assume breakouts can be staffed light because the GS team will float — they can't and they won't.

The honest sanity check

An old rule of thumb: budget roughly $30–$50 of labor per attendee for a standard mid plenary, scaling up to $80–$120 per attendee for a streaming-heavy large plenary. If your producer's budget per attendee is below $25, you are either looking at a Tier 1 show or you are about to discover what gets cut.

If a vendor quotes 40% below this range, ask which roles they have stripped. The answer usually surprises the producer.

How to use this on a real estimate

Three steps:

  1. Pick your tier by attendee count. That sets the baseline crew sheet.
  2. Apply the multipliers your show actually has (streaming, rehearsal, talent mgmt, concurrent breakouts). Each one bumps crew count or hours.
  3. Adjust 5–15% for venue complexity (loading-dock access, freight elevator constraints, union vs. non-union floor).

Then call us. We do this math on every estimate and we are happy to walk producers through it role by role on a discovery call. The number on the estimate is the number on the invoice; the math is the math.
