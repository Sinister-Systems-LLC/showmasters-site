Why we pay our riggers
more than competitors.

Published May 23, 2026 7 min read Show Masters Editorial

We pay our Lead Riggers 18–25% above the regional event-rigging market rate. We do it on purpose. Producers ask about it occasionally; riggers thinking about applying ask about it constantly. Here is the math, and why it pencils out for the company, the rigger, and the producer all at once.

The role nobody else wants to underprice

A rigger working a load-in is 20 feet in the air on a chain motor, hands on a piece of $40,000 truss with three points of attachment to the venue. The job demands physical strength, situational awareness, decision-making under fatigue, and zero tolerance for shortcut work. One missed pin or one mis-counted point load is the kind of mistake the industry remembers.

The bench of people who can do this job well is small. The bench of people who can do it for fifteen years is smaller. Every regional market has roughly 30–60 working Lead Riggers, and the senior ones know each other by first name. That market is small and price-sensitive; when one company's rate goes up, the bench notices.

What we pay

In 2026, a Lead Rigger at Show Masters earns:

  • $72–$95/hr on a standard arena call — 18–25% above the regional non-union average.
  • +$8/hr ETCP premium for current ETCP-Rigger certification (we pay for renewal every 5 years).
  • +$6/hr SPRAT premium for current SPRAT Level 1+ rope-access certification.
  • +$10/hr stadium / festival premium for shows above arena scale.
  • Full benefits — workers' comp (with rigging explicitly on the policy, not excluded), $2M GL coverage, ACA-compliant health for full-time crew, paid pre-call walk-throughs.

The senior end of the Lead Rigger bench (the riggers with 12+ years and stadium-scale credits) regularly clears six-figure annual W-2 wages with us. Not because we run them sixty hours a week (we don't), but because the hourly rate plus specialty premiums add up across a steady book of shows.

Why we pay it

Five honest reasons:

1. We can't afford the alternative

A rigging mistake is the single most expensive failure mode in our business. Property damage at venue scale, injury claims, OSHA filings, the kind of incident that ends partnerships. The price difference between a Lead Rigger we are confident in and a Lead Rigger we are nervous about is, conservatively, the difference between sleeping at night and not.

2. Producers ask for the same names again

Repeat producers don't book a company — they book the Lead Rigger. We can't deliver that continuity if our senior riggers leave for the company down the street paying $12 more per hour. Paying premium keeps the bench stable, and stable bench is the entire business model.

3. Certifications cost real money to maintain

ETCP-Rigger recertification is ~$500 every 5 years. SPRAT Level 1 is similar. OSHA-30 is annual. We pay these for crew because the certifications matter to the work — and because a rigger who knows the company pays for their certs has one less reason to leave.

4. The math says it works

An $8/hr premium across a 40-hour rigging week is $320/week or about $16,000/yr per rigger. Multiply by our 18-rigger Lead bench and the premium costs the company roughly $290,000 annually. Across our book of business, that's about 1.4% of revenue. Versus the cost of an incident, a senior departure, or a producer choosing a competitor, it's the cheapest insurance policy in the company.

5. It's the right thing to do

Riggers work at altitude in physical conditions for shifts that often exceed twelve hours. The compensation should reflect the actual difficulty and risk of the work. It often doesn't, across the industry. We have an opportunity to be different here, and we take it.

Producers ask whether the higher rigger rate gets passed to them. Yes — it's in the line item. They pay for it. They also stop having rigging conversations they'd rather not have. Net positive for them.

How to get on the rigger bench

If you're an experienced rigger with current ETCP-R or SPRAT certification (or willing to test for it on our budget), apply through the careers page. We hire from related disciplines too — industrial rope access, ironworker, ski-lift mechanic, broadcast-tower — with a clear apprenticeship path to entertainment rigging. We pay you while you train. We pay you to recertify. The bench rotates senior Lead Riggers into mentorship roles for new hires, so you'll be working alongside someone who has been doing the job for a decade.
