Trade show I&D in Orlando.
A producer's labor checklist.

Published Apr 7, 2026 11 min read Show Masters Editorial

Orlando crews ~750 trade shows a year across three big venues: Orange County Convention Center, OCC at the Marriott World Center, and Gaylord Palms. Each has different rules, different freight access, different unions on the floor, and different ways to lose money if you've never run a show here. This checklist is what we hand every first-time Orlando exhibitor on the discovery call.

Read it before you book the truck.

The three venues, by the numbers

Orange County Convention Center (OCCC). 7 million sq ft, two campuses (North/South). Largest convention venue in Florida. Move-in is freight-driveway-based; trucks queue and dock by appointment. Union floor for general show services (Centerplate, etc.), but I&D labor is producer's choice — we crew the dismantle for many shows running side-by-side with the union GSC.

Orange County Center (OCC, Marriott World Center). Mid-sized. Hotel-attached, so freight is via the hotel loading dock and a single freight elevator to the exhibit hall. Non-union floor. Move-in windows are tight because the dock is shared with hotel deliveries.

Gaylord Palms. Resort venue, exhibit hall plus ballroom inventory. Non-union. Freight is via a discrete service road behind the convention wing. Loading dock is small relative to the hall, so I&D timing is the constraint that drives everything else.

Move-in windows and what they actually look like

A move-in window is the period the venue's loading dock is open to your show. For a typical 3-day trade show, the window pattern is:

  • Day -2: Targeted move-in begins for large exhibits (usually 8am-6pm with break for venue dinner shift).
  • Day -1: General move-in, smaller booths, hanging signage and rigging finalization.
  • Day 0 morning: Final-touch, AV ready-call, badge scanners online.
  • Show days: No move-in.
  • Show close +0 to +24: Move-out begins immediately at show close. This is the tightest window.

The mistake almost every first-time Orlando producer makes: assuming move-out runs at the same pace as move-in. It doesn't. Move-out is faster, denser, and more dangerous because everyone wants out at once. We staff move-out at ~1.4x the crew count of move-in on the same booth, every time.

Union vs. non-union: what it actually changes

OCCC has union show-services workers for catering, decorating, and electrical. They're great. But I&D labor — moving freight, building booths, breaking them down — is not jurisdictional. Producers pick their crew. We're non-union and bill non-union rates for this work, which is typically 18-25% under the union equivalent for the same skill level.

OCC and Gaylord Palms are fully non-union. No jurisdictional restrictions on who does what; you pick the right crew and they do all the work.

The "union venue" label sounds intimidating to first-time producers but rarely binds I&D. Ask specifically what's jurisdictional before you assume your hands are tied.

Freight elevators, gear cages, and the floor plan

Three things determine whether your I&D runs smoothly or runs into hour 16:

1. Freight elevator scheduling

At OCC and Gaylord Palms, freight moves through one or two elevators. Slots are booked in 30-minute windows. If your move-in is scheduled for 10am-12pm and the elevator slot was given to a competing booth at 10am, you wait. Book the elevator before you book the crew. Our Crew Leads coordinate elevator slots with the venue's freight desk on every Orlando show.

2. Gear cage placement

Where your road cases live during the show drives every minute of move-in and move-out. The right placement is close to your booth, behind a closing curtain, with a clear path to the loading dock. The wrong placement is 300 feet from your booth, across the registration concourse, with one elevator between you and the truck. Gear cage placement is a thirty-second conversation with the venue's I&D coordinator. Have it the day you sign the venue contract, not the day before move-in.

3. Floor plan flow

If your booth is in the back of the hall, the crew walks past everyone else's booth on every trip. That adds up. At a 30-booth move-out, that's an extra 90 minutes of foot time over the day, which is 2-3 crew-hours per crew member. We push every Orlando producer to think about flow during the booth-pick conversation, not after.

The four mistakes that cost most first-time Orlando exhibitors

1. Hiring on the day of the show

Orlando trade-show season is busy. Show weeks at OCCC sometimes see 80% of available labor booked four to six weeks ahead. Producers who call us the Monday before a Wednesday move-in usually get crew, but they pay a 10-15% short-notice premium, and they don't get their preferred Crew Lead. Book six weeks out. Move-in dates lock as soon as the venue contract is signed; the labor call should land in the next phone call after that.

2. Skipping the venue walk-through

OCCC South vs. North is a 12-minute drive. OCC's exhibit hall is on the upper level of the Marriott. Gaylord Palms has multiple loading docks depending on which hall the show is in. A 45-minute walk-through with the Crew Lead the day before move-in catches most of the surprises. We do this for every first-time Orlando client at no charge.

3. Underestimating move-out crew

Already mentioned, worth saying twice. The clock at move-out is the venue's, not yours. Most Orlando venues charge overtime on dock access if you miss the window. We staff move-out at 1.4x the move-in count and finish on time. The math is cheaper than the overtime.

4. Treating I&D as a single line item

I&D is not one crew over one shift. It's a freight-handling team at the dock, a rigging team for hanging signage, a build team for booth assembly, and a finish team for AV/electrical hookup. Each has a different rate and a different size. A single blended "I&D rate" estimate hides the math. Ours always breaks it out so you see exactly what's being staffed.

What to ask any vendor before you book

  • Have you crewed this specific venue in the last 12 months?
  • Which Crew Lead will run my call, and what's their track record at OCCC / OCC / Gaylord?
  • Will you do a pre-show venue walk-through with my team?
  • How do you scale move-out crew relative to move-in?
  • What's your protocol if the freight elevator slot slips?

Specific answers mean someone who has actually run trade-show I&D in Orlando. Vague answers mean they've read about it on a venue spec sheet.
