The terms your crew
uses on show day.

If you've ever heard a Crew Lead say "rig the beamer at the dead-hang point and run a five-pin to the FOH dimmer" and nodded — this page is for the rest of us. 50+ stage and production terms, plain English.

ABCDE FGHIL MPRST VW

A

AVL
Audio · Video · Lighting. The three technical disciplines that show up on most production briefs. Producers will say "I need AVL crew" to mean "I need techs across all three departments."
Apron
The part of the stage that extends forward of the proscenium arch (toward the audience). Lighting + spot crews work different cues in the apron than upstage.

B

Beam (rigger)
An overhead structural steel beam in a venue. "Up-riggers on the beam" means riggers physically clip motors to the venue's overhead steel to hang truss and scenery.
Beamer (Beam Projector)
A lighting fixture that throws a tight, parallel beam of light — used for hard-edged shafts of light, often visible in haze. Distinct from a wash or spot fixture.
Black box theater
A flexible-seating venue with black walls + ceiling, no fixed proscenium. Common in PACs (performing arts centers) for experimental theater.

C

Call sheet
The single document that tells every crew member where to be and when, who's running each department, what's loading where, the show-day timing.
Call time
The time a crew member is required to be on site, ready to work. "Call is 0500 for load-in" = show up at 5am.
COI (Certificate of Insurance)
The piece of paper that proves your crewing partner carries the insurance you've been told they carry. Issued to the venue. SMPL provides COIs free, usually within one business day.
Cue
A scripted moment in the show that triggers a technical action — light cue, sound cue, video cue. Called by the Stage Manager.
Cyc / Cyclorama
A large fabric backdrop, usually white or sky-blue, stretched at the back of the stage. Lit by cyc lights to create depth or "open sky" effect.

D

Dead-hang
A static attachment point — a load that doesn't move once flown. Contrast with motor-rigged where the truss can be raised/lowered with hoists.
Deck (deck hand)
The actual stage floor. "Deck hands" work the deck during a show — moving scenery, handling props, setting up the next cue.
Dimmer
The electronic device between a power source and a lighting fixture that controls brightness. Older venues have dimmer racks; modern LED fixtures are usually self-dimming.
DMX
The digital control protocol that lighting consoles use to talk to fixtures. DMX-512 carries 512 channels per "universe." Big shows run 50+ universes.

E

ETC / ETCP
Two different acronyms. ETC = Electronic Theater Controls, a console manufacturer. ETCP = Entertainment Technician Certification Program, the rigging certification SMPL up-riggers carry.

F

FOH (Front of House)
The audience side of the show. FOH audio = mix position out in the audience. FOH lighting = follow-spots and lights aimed from the audience toward the stage.
Fly system / Flies
The overhead system that raises and lowers scenery, drops, and lighting battens. Found in proper theaters with full grid + counterweight rigging.
Follow spot
A high-powered spotlight that a human operator manually tracks across the performer. The "follow" part is what makes it a follow spot.

G

Gel
A colored sheet placed in front of a lighting fixture to tint the light. Modern LED fixtures generate color directly so gels are increasingly historical.
Gobo
A patterned metal or glass disc placed in front of a lighting fixture to project a shape (logo, leaves, pattern) instead of a plain beam.

H

Hang
To attach a fixture to a truss or batten. "Hang the back-light truss" = mount the lighting fixtures onto the back truss before flying it.
House (the house)
The audience seating area. "Open the house" = let the audience in. "House lights" = lights over the audience.

I

I&D (Install & Dismantle)
Trade-show specific term. The crew that installs the exhibit booths before the show and tears them down after.
IMAG (Image Magnification)
Live video of the performer projected on large screens flanking or behind the stage so the back of the room can see them. Common at corporate plenaries.

L

Load-in / Load-out
The process of bringing all gear into the venue + setting it up (load-in), then breaking it down + getting it out (load-out). Often the most crew-intensive parts of a show.
Lavalier (lav)
A small clip-on microphone worn on the body. Used on speakers, presenters, theatrical performers. "Get the speaker mic'd up — lav them."

M

Mic (mic'd up)
Microphone. To "mic up" a performer = clip a lav, hand them a handheld, or fit them with an over-ear.
MA (grandMA)
A specific brand of lighting console used on most large concert + corporate shows. Operating a grandMA is its own discipline.

P

PAC (Performing Arts Center)
A purpose-built theater complex, often civic. SMPL holds in-house crewing contracts at several PACs.
Patch / Patching
Connecting a control channel (on a lighting or audio console) to a specific physical fixture or output. "Patch the back-light to channel 47."
Pipe-and-drape
A modular system of vertical pipes + horizontal cross-bars + hanging fabric used to create temporary walls, masking, or backdrops. Trade shows live and die on pipe-and-drape.
Plot (lighting plot)
The technical drawing that shows every lighting fixture's position, focus, color, and channel. The lighting designer's bible.
Proscenium
The arch at the front of a traditional theater stage that frames the action. Defines where "on stage" ends and "the house" begins.

R

Rigger
The crew member responsible for hanging things overhead — truss, motors, scenery, video walls, lighting fixtures. Up-riggers climb; ground-riggers hand things up.
Run of show
The detailed timeline of every moment in the show — when each speaker walks on, every video, every cue. The Stage Manager owns this document.

S

Scrim
A semi-transparent fabric backdrop. Lit from the front it looks opaque; lit from behind it becomes see-through. Used for theatrical reveals.
Spot (spot op)
Short for "follow spot" or "follow-spot operator."
SPRAT
Society of Professional Rope Access Technicians. A rope-access certification SMPL up-riggers may carry alongside ETCP.
Stagehand
The general live-event labor role. Stagehands load in, set up, run cues during the show, and load out. The fundamental crew position.
Strike
To tear down. "Strike the stage" after the show ends = load-out begins.

T

Tech Director (TD)
The senior crew lead who runs the technical side of a production end-to-end. Sits between the producer and the department leads.
Truss
The aluminum lattice structure that holds lighting fixtures + speakers in the air over the stage. Comes in box, triangle, and circular shapes.
Turnaround
The process of resetting a stage between shows in a multi-show day. Tight turnarounds are crew-intensive.

V

Video wall
A grid of small LED panels assembled into one large display. Modern corporate plenaries often use 40+ panel walls.

W

W-4 (employee)
An IRS form that classifies a worker as an EMPLOYEE (not a contractor). SMPL hires W-4 only. Distinguishes from 1099 contractors. See /insurance.
Wedge
A floor monitor speaker that aims back at the performers so they can hear themselves. The classic "wedge in front of the mic stand" on a concert stage.
Wrap
End of the show day. "That's a wrap" = we're done. Often followed by strike.
